From the Kitchen to Google Ireland

The honest version — including the part where I washed dishes for a living.

Luiz Sousa April 2026 · 8 min read

I've been thinking about writing this for a while.

Not because I think my story is special. But because I keep meeting people — Brazilians in Dublin, professionals stuck between where they are and where they want to be — who ask me how I made the jump. And I always give them the condensed version because the full one takes a while.

This is the full one.

The Job I Didn't Get

I was working at Seiko Epson in Brazil. Marketing across Latin America — digital strategy, campaigns, the kind of work I'd been building toward for years. I was good at it, the team knew I was good at it, and when a Digital Marketing Specialist role came up at Epson Americas, I thought: this is it.

I got through the initial stages. And then came an interview — conducted in English.

I survived it. But just. The language wasn't where it needed to be, and whoever was on the other side of the call could tell. I didn't get the job.

I remember sitting with that for a while. Not with the self-pity version of it — more the analytical version. What actually happened here? Was I not good enough?

No. I was good enough at the job. I just didn't have the tool to prove it in that room. That distinction matters. Because it told me exactly what to fix.

Asking to Be Fired

Here's the part that still makes people raise an eyebrow.

I went to Epson and asked them to let me go.

Not because I was unhappy. Not because I had something lined up. In Brazil, being formally dismissed rather than resigning gives you access to FGTS — the government severance fund every employed worker contributes to throughout their career. That money was my runway. Without it, what I was planning to do was impossible. With it, I had a shot.

They agreed. I left with enough to take one real risk.

I landed in Dublin in 2016 with functional English, a CV that meant nothing to anyone in the Irish job market, and no idea how long the money would last.

The Kitchen

Let me be straight about this part because it's the part people find hardest to say out loud.

The first job I got in Ireland was washing dishes.

Not a marketing role. Not even an admin role with a vague marketing adjacency. A kitchen. Actual food. Actual dishes. The invisible work that keeps a restaurant running that most people with a university degree and a decade of professional experience would quietly leave off their LinkedIn.

I'm not going to romanticise it. It was humbling in a specific way that's hard to describe — being invisible in a professional sense, in a country where nobody knows your name or your context. In Brazil I had a track record. In Dublin I had a rubber apron.

But I was also doing something those years in Brazil couldn't give me: I was being surrounded by English every day, with no option to retreat. No Portuguese colleagues to fall back on. No way to half-participate and get away with it.

The language was being built the only way it actually works — by necessity.

What I Was Doing at Night

While the kitchen paid the rent, other things were happening.

Freelance marketing work. Small clients, modest fees, but real briefs in English, real deliverables, building a portfolio that could prove I could operate in a market that didn't know my name. Certifications. Courses. Lots of job applications — not sent randomly, but researched, targeted, written in English I kept getting better at.

I want to be honest about the timeline here because the condensed version makes it sound faster than it was. This was years of parallel work. The kitchen wasn't a brief detour — it was the day job while the real work happened everywhere else.

The Google Kitchen

In March 2019 I got a role as a kitchen porter at Google Ireland.

I want to be precise about what that was: a kitchen porter contract on the Google campus. Not a marketing role. Not an internal transfer. The same work I'd been doing, in the same building where people were making decisions about advertising strategy for half of Europe.

What I learned in that building I couldn't have learned anywhere else.

I learned how the culture actually felt from the inside. The way people talked about problems. What mattered and what didn't. The difference between how a platform presents itself to advertisers and how it actually operates when you're in the building.

I never stopped interviewing. Every conversation was research. And I never made the mistake of thinking the kitchen was where the story was going to end.

Mindshare

Before Google called, Mindshare did.

One of the world's largest media agencies. A proper strategy role. The kind of position where my background — Epson, LATAM markets, digital advertising — finally had a frame around it that people in Dublin understood and valued.

This was the moment I understood what had actually happened over the previous three years. The gap was never capability. It was context. And context — knowing how the market works, how to present yourself in it, how to operate in a different professional culture — can be built. It just takes longer than you want it to.

The Call

The Google Ireland offer came in May 2022.

Senior Digital Marketing Strategist. EMEA scope. Working with some of the largest advertisers in Europe, advising on the exact platforms I'd once been trying to get a job at from the outside.

I thought about that interview at Epson Americas. The one where my English wasn't where it needed to be. I thought about the kitchen in 2016, about the freelance projects late at night, about the morning I sent my first application to Google's strategy team while still working their kitchen shift.

Six years. One bet. No guarantee it was going to work.

What I Actually Learned

I work with people now who are at different versions of where I was. Some are Brazilian professionals who want to work in Europe and don't know how the market actually works. Some are marketers who can see where they want to get to but not how. Some just need someone who's been inside the room to tell them what's true.

A few things I know for certain now that I wish I'd known in 2016:

  • The gap is almost never what it looks like on the surface. Mine looked like English. It was actually market context — understanding how to position yourself in a different professional culture, how hiring works, what people are actually looking for.
  • Parallel work compounds. Every freelance project, every certification, every rejected application taught me something I used in the next one. None of it felt like it was adding up at the time. It was.
  • The humbling part isn't optional. Being in that building, even from the edges of it, changed how I operated. Proximity to the culture you want to be part of matters.
  • Start before you're ready. I wasn't ready when I landed in Dublin. The readiness came from doing it anyway.
"The gap is almost never what you think it is."
